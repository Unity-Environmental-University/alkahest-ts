The materials

Three states of matter

Every node in your dependency graph has a phase — its intrinsic material type. This is what it is, independent of what's happening right now.

Phase is material. Temperature is weather.

A Salt is crystalline — it precipitates once and just becomes TypeScript. A Fluid flows: it's generated, but re-flows if the compiler breaks it. A Volatile evaporates and re-precipitates on every run. It never settles.

The thermodynamics

Heat comes from change

When you change a Salt — a type change, a restructure — it releases a burst of heat. When a Fluid recompiles or breaks, it emits a smaller pulse. That heat doesn't stay put.

Heat flows outward through the dependency graph — from things others depend on, toward the things that depend on them. A locked-in core node has high thermal mass: lots of dependents, high boiling point. Edge nodes — with nothing depending on them — are low mass. They absorb heat easily and boil first.

The more locked in place something is, the further its heat can dissipate to the edges.

And it cools over time — not clock time, but changelog time. Each commit that passes without touching a node lets it cool a little more. Leave something alone for long enough and it crystallizes back toward salt behavior, regardless of what it is.

The math

Thermal mass from the graph

Thresholds aren't set by hand — they're calculated from the graph topology. A node with many dependents has high thermal mass: it takes more heat to change its effective behavior. A leaf node with no dependents has minimal mass and boils at low temperatures.

Event Heat emitted Why
Salt type change 2.5 Crystalline structures release energy violently when broken
Fluid recompile / break 1.0 Fluids are already in motion — less energy to shift them
Propagation decay × 0.6 per edge Heat attenuates as it travels — distant nodes warm less
Cooling per commit − 0.4 Changelog time: each untouched commit lets a node cool
The insight

Your git log is the thermal history

Because heat is measured in commits, you can replay your entire changelog as a thermal simulation. Feed in every file touch, emit the right kind of heat, step the automaton forward.

What you get is a thermal map of your codebase over time. Hot zones are areas of recent churn. Cold crystallized zones are stable, untouched, high-confidence. Phase transition events — moments when effective phase flipped — are the risky commits.

The cross-cut edges are especially interesting: level-loader → wave-controller means a level schema change sends heat across subsystem boundaries into the entity spawning system. base-model → grunt/carrier/boss means touching the shared MVC base simultaneously scorches every enemy type's model layer. These are the edges you didn't know you had to worry about.

You can also run it forward: touch a salt node and watch what lights up downstream.

The phase markers (Volatile, Fluid, Salt) stay as type annotations in your TypeScript. Temperature is extrinsic — it lives outside the code, derived from history. The two together tell you both what something is and what it's doing right now.

Matter has memory

The same node can behave like a gas or a crystal depending on how recently you've been touching it. That's not a metaphor — it's the model.

On phase markers

Intrinsic vs extrinsic

Phase markers in alkahest (Volatile, Fluid, Salt) are TypeScript interfaces you implement. They describe the nature of a type: how it was generated and how it behaves during dissolution.

Temperature is different — it's not in the code at all. It's derived from changelog history and lives in the thermal graph at runtime. The same node can have the same phase marker but wildly different effective behavior depending on how hot it currently is.

This mirrors physical matter: ice is always H₂O, but whether it's solid or liquid depends on conditions, not composition.
